Looking after your soldering iron

Without a clean soldering iron, you will always struggle to make a good joint.

The tip of your iron, when hot, should be bright silver. If it is dull and brown, follow these steps to clean and tin it.

  1. Heat up your iron.
  2. Clean any deposits off the tip using brass wool or a wet sponge.
  3. Melt some solder onto the tip, then it clean off using your brass wool or sponge.
  4. The flux in the centre of your solder will clean the tip, and the solder itself will tin it.
  5. Repeat this process a couple of times if necessary, to get a bright tip.
  6. If this is not working, you can use tip tinner or cleaner. This is a mixture of flux and solder, especially designed for this purpose.
  7. Push your hot iron into the paste, then wipe clean with your brass wool or sponge.

When you are finished, your iron's tip should be bright silver.

When you have finished soldering, repeat steps 2 and 3 before turning off your iron.

This will keep the iron clean, making it easier to use in the future.

If your soldering iron is old, or you have used it a lot, it might not clean effectively anymore. In this case, it might be time to buy a new tip for your iron.

Soldering your snowflake - step-by-step guide

Collect your components:

  • A: 1x snowflake circuit board
  • B: 4x LEDs, your choice of colour
  • C: 1x battery holder
  • D: 4x resistors, your choice of values (see next step)
  • E: 1x slide switch

You will also need a CR2032 coin cell battery. If you are in Science Oxford's workshop, this will be given to you when you are ready to test your circuit board.

Collect your tools:

  • Soldering iron
  • Wet sponge
  • Solder
  • Heat-proof surface
  • Blue tack
  • Wire snippers

Step 1: resistors

  • Test out resistor values on Tinkercad, and choose the most appropriate ones for your LEDs.
  • Push your resistors into the holes labelled R1, R2, R3, R4 on your circuit board.
  • Once they are pushed through, bend the legs outwards slightly so that they don't fall out.
  • Solder all eight connections.
  • Trim down the legs using the wire snippers by holding the wire with one hand, and snipping with the other - this is to stop the bits of wire from pinging away!

Step 2: battery holder

  • On the back of the snowflake is a circle, with the label 'melt solder here' on it. Melting a thin layer of solder onto this circle will help to create a good connection for your battery. If you are in Science Oxford's workshop, this has already been done for you.
  • Line your battery holder up with the holes on the PCB, and push it all the way in until it is secure and flush with the board.
  • Turn the board over and solder the two battery holder connections.

Step 3: switch

  • You can attach the switch either on the front or back of the board - your choice. Push it in your chosen direction, and use some blue tack to hold it in place.
  • Solder all three legs of the switch then, once it has cooled down, remove the blue tack.
  • Don't trim down the legs of the switch, as they are too short to hold in place and cut safely. Once you have finished the whole circuit board, you can cover up the sharp edges.

Step 4: LEDs

  • Unlike the other components we have used so far, LEDs will only work if they are soldered into the board the correct way round.
  • For every LED:
    1. Match it with its resistor (if you have different values) to decide which of the four spaces it should go in.
    2. Identify the negative leg of your LED - this is the short leg, and line this up with the square hole on the circuit board.
    3. Push your LED all the way in, then double-check that the short leg is in the square hole, and the long leg in the round hole.
    4. Bend the legs outwards slightly to stop it from falling out.
  • Solder all eight connections.
  • Don't trim down the legs yet - you are going to test it first!

Step 5: testing

  • Check all of your solder joints to make sure they look good. If you are in Science Oxford's workshop, ask a workshop leader to check it.
  • Push a CR2032 coin cell battery into the battery holder - you need to push quite hard to get it all the way in.
  • Test your board - do all of the LEDs come on? Do they turn on and off when you slide the switch?
  • If your circuit doesn't work as expected, make sure that none of the LED legs are touching each other, and check your solder joints again.
  • If your circuit works, then you can trim the legs of the LEDs.

Step 6: finishing touches

Now that your snowflake lights up, you can add some more personal touches! Some suggestions:

  • Choose a ribbon or string to hang it with.
  • Cover any sharp wires with a hot glue gun or some foam tape.
  • Decorate the white surface with marker pens.
